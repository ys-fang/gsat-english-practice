第35至38題

Urban farming, also known as urban agriculture, is the practice of growing food in urban areas such as cities and towns. This innovative approach to food production has gained significant popularity in recent years as cities around the world face challenges related to food security, environmental sustainability, and population growth.

There are several types of urban farming, including rooftop gardens, vertical farms, community gardens, and hydroponic systems. Each method has its own advantages and can be adapted to different urban environments. Rooftop gardens, for example, make use of unused space on building tops, while vertical farms maximize production in limited space by growing crops in vertically stacked layers.

The benefits of urban farming are numerous. First, it reduces the distance food needs to travel from farm to plate, which decreases transportation costs and carbon emissions. Second, it provides fresh, locally grown produce to urban residents, improving food security and nutrition. Third, urban farming can create jobs and stimulate local economic development. Finally, it helps improve air quality and provides green spaces that enhance the urban environment.

However, urban farming also faces several challenges. Limited space, high land costs, and soil contamination in urban areas can make it difficult to establish farming operations. Additionally, urban farmers often lack the technical knowledge and resources needed to succeed. Government support and community involvement are essential for overcoming these obstacles and promoting sustainable urban agriculture.

第39至42題

Sleep is one of the most important aspects of human health, yet many people underestimate its significance. During sleep, our bodies undergo crucial processes that help maintain physical health, mental well-being, and cognitive function. Research has shown that consistently getting adequate sleep is essential for optimal performance in daily activities.

The human sleep cycle consists of several stages, including light sleep, deep sleep, and REM (Rapid Eye Movement) sleep. Each stage serves different functions. Light sleep helps the body transition from wakefulness to deeper sleep stages. Deep sleep is crucial for physical recovery, tissue repair, and immune system strengthening. REM sleep, during which most dreaming occurs, is vital for memory consolidation, learning, and emotional regulation.

Unfortunately, modern lifestyle factors often interfere with healthy sleep patterns. Excessive screen time, particularly before bedtime, can disrupt the body's natural circadian rhythm by suppressing the production of melatonin, a hormone that regulates sleep. Stress, caffeine consumption, irregular work schedules, and environmental factors like noise and light pollution can also negatively impact sleep quality.

The consequences of chronic sleep deprivation are far-reaching. Short-term effects include decreased concentration, impaired judgment, mood swings, and reduced reaction time. Long-term sleep loss has been linked to serious health conditions such as obesity, diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and weakened immune function. Additionally, insufficient sleep can significantly impact academic and work performance, leading to decreased productivity and increased error rates.

Fortunately, there are several strategies for improving sleep quality. Establishing a consistent sleep schedule, creating a comfortable sleep environment, limiting screen time before bed, and practicing relaxation techniques can all contribute to better sleep. Regular exercise, while beneficial for sleep, should be avoided close to bedtime as it can be stimulating. If sleep problems persist, consulting a healthcare professional may be necessary to identify and address underlying issues.

第43至46題

The concept of sustainable tourism has emerged as a crucial approach to balancing the economic benefits of travel with the need to protect natural environments and local communities. As global tourism continues to grow rapidly, with billions of people traveling internationally each year, the industry faces increasing pressure to address its environmental and social impacts.

Traditional mass tourism often leads to overcrowding, environmental degradation, and cultural commodification in popular destinations. Famous landmarks and natural sites frequently suffer from the effects of too many visitors, including pollution, erosion, and disruption of local ecosystems. Additionally, the economic benefits of tourism may not always reach local communities, instead flowing primarily to large international corporations.

Sustainable tourism offers an alternative model that aims to minimize negative impacts while maximizing positive contributions to destinations. This approach emphasizes responsible travel practices, such as choosing eco-friendly accommodations, supporting local businesses, respecting cultural traditions, and minimizing waste and resource consumption. Sustainable tourism also promotes education and awareness, helping travelers understand and appreciate the places they visit.

Several strategies can help promote sustainable tourism development. Destination management organizations can implement visitor limits, develop alternative attractions to distribute tourist traffic, and invest in infrastructure that supports both tourism and local needs. Governments can create policies that encourage sustainable practices and provide incentives for businesses that adopt environmentally friendly operations. Meanwhile, travelers themselves play a crucial role by making conscious choices about where to go, how to travel, and which businesses to support.

The future of tourism depends largely on the industry's ability to embrace sustainability. Climate change, environmental degradation, and social inequality pose significant challenges that require immediate action. By working together, tourism stakeholders can create a more responsible and sustainable industry that benefits both visitors and host communities for generations to come.
